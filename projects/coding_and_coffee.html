Coding and Coffee

Good Intentions Don't Ship

c. 2018-2020

The Origin Story

Freshman year at UNL, I took CSCE155A -- the first CS course in the program. A "weed-out" class. I passed with a B+ and felt like I learned nothing. I took it again the following semester, passed with a C, and for the first time, actually understood what was happening.

I started asking the students who seemed to genuinely breeze through it what their secret was. There was no secret. The answer was always the same: they had taken CS in high school. Some had been coding for years before they set foot in the classroom.

It wasn't that they were smarter. It was that their schools had given them a head start. And at the time -- before vibe coding -- software engineering was one of the most reliably lucrative fields you could enter — and a lot of underfunded schools simply didn't have access to that curriculum. It felt like an enormous thing to just accept.

I didn't accept it.

The Master Plan

In my head, the plan was straightforward: show up to high schools with curriculum and teach students. Give them what their schools hadn't. Close the gap.

I outlined an elaborate plan, a manifesto of sorts, that passionately detailed a five-phase Computer Science curriculum, in Java (because "java"... "coffee" get it? So clever), separate tracks for schools with and without existing CS programs, a coffee shop partnership network, resume workshops, algorithm/technical interview preparation, guaranteed internships at companies post-program, and a reward system where students earned stickers, mugs, t-shirts and more for completing each phase.

The Creative Liberties

The proposal described existing interest from coffee shops. The actual truth is I had run the idea by a cute barista I was trying to impress. She said it sounded cool. I recorded that as interest. If you squint, it's not entirely a lie...

The guaranteed internships post-program -- that wording might have been a stretch. Those were intentions I had not yet acted on. The proposal did not fully distinguish between the two.

I had nothing but good intentions... the document just occasionally presented those intentions as infrastructure.

Twelve Pages and a Dream

I printed copies, marked all the coffee shops on a map, and started pitching.

I didn't start small. Starbucks, Scooters, the big regional chains. After all, I'm from Texas -- go big or go home. They all sent me home.

I pivoted my stragety. I went smaller. Local shops, independent owners. Straight to the source. Some handed me a black coffee on the way out... but a few actually listened. And after enough of those conversations, I started getting real interest.

Not commitments. Interest.

Then the goalpost moved. The people who were genuinely curious stopped being satisfied with just the paper plan. They wanted proof. A curriculum mockup. Something that looked like a real program and not a very passionate document written at 2am.

The vision was real, but a vision needs a body. And it turns out, there is a significant amount of infrastructure required before a school will allow you anywhere near their students — understandably so. You need institutional backing, cleared mentors, liability considerations, administrative approval. I had none of that.

The Logo

The first thing a real program needs is a face. What I didn't know yet was that the logo would become my first real lesson in what it means to pay for work.

I started with friends and young designers trying to build their portfolios. I was grateful for every one of them — genuinely. They gave their time to something that wasn't paying basically anything, and that meant a lot. Just none of them were quite right. Not bad -- something was always just a little off from what I had in my head. I started used an early iteration that was "good enough," but it ate at me.

The problem was I couldn't tell anyone what was in my head, because I didn't know. I had never been on this side of the table before — the one doing the asking, trying to articulate a vision I couldn't yet describe. I just knew what it wasn't. After all, it's always easier to know what you don't want -- than what you do want.

I had become obsessed. People started telling me I was spending way too much time on something that didn't matter. And maybe to them it didn't, but the logo had become a personal pursuit -- it was the face of something I had poured everything into -- and getting it wrong felt like getting the whole thing wrong. Nonetheless, I was investing money and time into something I hadn't validated would be worth it. I understood the optics, and I was getting nverous.

Eventually, I got connected with a professional design firm willing to work pro bono — courtesy of my ex-girlfriend's best friend's sister. Score. A real firm. This was going to be it. I eagerly waited...

When it came back, it was higher-quality than anything I had seen... I was beyond grateful. However, it wasn't it. I felt genuinely lost.

Logo iterations

From the deep depths of Reddit, a student in Massachusetts reached out and said they had a vision for it. They definitely did.

Final Logo

After the logo landed, something strange happened. But before I go into that, there's a detail I need to walk through:

The first thing I wanted to do was put the logo on everything. My program advisor connected me with the CS department's marketing contact, who came back with a quote for shirts. I had raised a little funding but knew I was working on a tight budget. The numbers weren't impossible — until I thought harder about what I actually wanted.

You know those shirts you get for free and never wear? The ones that sit at the bottom of a drawer until you use them to wash your car? I would have been horrified if that became the Coding and Coffee shirt. Every program, every club, every organization was printing the same cheap shirts that no one actually wore. It felt completely backwards to spend money on something people would throw away.

I started going through my own drawers and looking at the tags. Gildan, over and over. They fit too tight, the cotton was thin, and no matter how good the design was, the shirt was never something you'd choose to wear.

I started thinking about what shirts people actually wore. And then it clicked — fraternities and sororities.

Hear me out. At any given moment on any college campus, you can find one: Comfort Colors, heavy ring-spun cotton, slightly oversized, worn in. Students sought those shirts out. They didn't end up washing cars.

I also noticed something else about the shirts that got worn vs. the ones that didn't. The free ones tried too hard — logos too big, too centered, too eager to be seen. They looked like advertisements. The ones people actually put on looked almost incidental. So I decided to go minimal. Small logo, chest placement. Let the shirt do the work.

The quotes I found for Comfort Colors were way out of budget. We were nonprofit as it was, and there was no way to make the numbers work through any of the standard channels I'd tried.

I printed copies, marked all the printing shops on a map, and started pitching.

I kept pushing on local printers until finally I found someone who got it, Nathan -- a genuinely good guy who saw what I was trying to do and offered to print at cost, no markup. I ordered 10 smalls, 20 mediums, 20 larges, 10 extra-larges.

By this point, I had spent a Nebraska fortune on what some saw as a "fancy logo and shirt" with no validation. The nerves grew.

Alas, the first ones went to the people who had shown up before there was anything to show up for. Early adopters.

Then the strange thing happened.

People started reaching out asking for a shirt. Not because I was handing them out — just because they had seen one. But I had a strict rule only early adopters, mentors, and students got shirts. I was showing up to fairs to get the program's name out -- shirts on display -- and walking away with sign-up sheets full of names. As the name Coding and Coffee started moving around in the department and campus, people were lining up get involved, even offering to buy the shirts.

Something I had been told repeatedly wasn't worth my time ended up paying out a dividend.

A lesson here could be: trust yourself when something feels right — even when everyone is saying it isn't.

However, I knew I had gambled and got lucky. All things considered, sometimes going with your gut is the right call -- or regret not having done so. You just better be right.

The First Real Partnerships

Soon after, I landed three partnerships that changed the shape of things.

The first was The Mill. They were interested — genuinely — but didn't have the space. Hosting a program four hours a week would eat into real estate they needed for their own operations. At the end of the day, business comes first, and I respected that.

What they offered instead was unlimited free coffee and permission to carry their brand. I could use their logo, reference the partnership, and show up to pitches with a well-known local name behind me. There was no money in it. But The Mill had weight in Lincoln, and I started putting that all over my materials.

The second was The Bay. They were in the middle of building out a collective space and specifically wanted to host programs in a new computer room — which also happened to double as a coffee shop and venue. It was exactly what Coffee & Coding needed on paper.

The third was the one that actually mattered.

Lincoln High School allowed me to start running an after-school program on their campus. Lincoln High was recognized as the lowest income school in Lincoln, Nebraska — and those were some tough kids. But tough and passionate aren't mutually exclusive. Hidden among the noise were kids from rough upbringings who genuinely wanted to get into something that could change the trajectory of their lives.

Meeting those kids was the whole point. Everything — the manifesto, the logo, the shirts, the pitches, the coffee shop partnerships — had been in service of getting in-front of those kids. After a lot of work, I was finally delivering impact to the people it was actually meant for.

Getting Mentors. Getting Money.

I realized the first batch of mentors mattered more than anything. These were the people who would set the tone for every student who walked through the door. Smart wasn't enough. They needed to be personable, patient, able to explain a concept five different times without making a sixteen-year-old feel stupid for not getting it the first time. The kind of person who could sit across from a kid from a who had every reason to check out — and make them want to stay.

I started thinking about the mentor requirements. Honors students. Former FANG experience. Real credentials that would signal to schools and sponsors that this wasn't just a passion project anymore. I wrote up the requirements, started advertising, and began to attract exactly the kind of talent I had hoped for.

For the record: I did not meet my own requirements.

Here's the thing — these people didn't just look good on a resume. They came with real ideas. I learned a lot about forming a good team from this. The good ones helped me significantly brush out the curriculum into something that could actually hold up in a room full of high schoolers.

It didn't take long before I'd raise a few thousand dollars. That number might not sound like much, but for perspective, my rent at the time was $300 a month and I was making $12 an hour as a software engineer for a lab. It was a Nebraska fortune.

The Routine

We had real backing from the community. A consistent group and schedule. Real stories from students chasing dreams. Full classrooms. 90% retention rate. Kids were showing up not just for the coding but for each other -- the in-person dynamic, the problems, the feeling of being part of something. I even managed to raise and pay an hourly rate to our mentors, Dom (now @Microsoft) and Ryan (now @Garmin).

I also learned high-schoolers don't really drink coffee. They like hot chocolate, but "Coding and Hot Choclate" doesn't have the same ring to it.

But finally, the thing that Coding and Coffee was always supposed to be at its core was a community. It was finally real.

2 years later, I was in deep conversations about funneling our students into local internships before they reached college. The original problem -- the gap I had lived through -- was finally being solved.

Then COVID hit.

Within weeks, we had to move everything remote. I immediately knew what that meant: the value proposition of the program lived in the classroom — the coffee, the mentors, the in-person energy. None of that translated to a Zoom link. Coding and Coffee shut down.

Years later, more formative than any class or internship, Coding and Coffee taught me what real impact feels like, what it costs to build it — and that sometimes, the world has other plans.
